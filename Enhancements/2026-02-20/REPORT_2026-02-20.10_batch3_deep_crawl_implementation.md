## Key Discoveries

### 1. Website Discovery is a Force Multiplier

The Louie Family Foundation had no website URL in IRS data and would have been analyzed on DB data alone (yielding POSSIBLE_FIT at best). Web search found louiefamilyfoundation.org, which revealed the WWII family military connection and explicit veteran preference, upgrading the verdict to STRONG_FIT. This is the second-best overall prospect found across all 53 foundations.

**Lesson:** Always run website discovery for no-URL foundations before analysis. The incremental effort (5 minutes of web searching) can reveal deal-changing information invisible in DB data.

### 2. The 501(c)(3) Ambiguity is a PF Eligibility Trap

Brach Foundation ($128M) states "Must be exempt under Sect 501c3" which technically includes PFs. However, context (all grantees are public charities, institutional funder with formal processes) makes clear this is shorthand for "public charity."

**Rule of thumb:** Bare "501(c)(3)" from large institutional funders = likely PF exclusion. From small family foundations = may genuinely include PFs.

### 3. Corporate Foundations are Uniformly NOT_A_FIT for PF Clients

All 3 corporate foundations in batch 3 (Cigna, Texas Instruments, First Interstate) were disqualified. This confirms the batch 2 finding and suggests corporate foundations should be pre-filtered out for PF clients.

### 4. The Howard Family Foundation is a "Sleeper" Lead

The unique combination of 5+ years of Judd Goldman Adaptive Sailing, Columbia Sailing School ($80K), AND Wounded Warrior Project funding makes this the most thematically aligned foundation discovered. The bank-managed trust structure is unusual but Grantable.co confirms open applications.

### 5. IRC 170(c) vs 509(a): Critical Legal Distinction

The Kellcie Fund requires "IRC Section 170(c)" which explicitly includes private foundations, while Crail-Johnson requires "509(a)(1) or 509(a)(2)" which explicitly excludes them. This legal distinction is the single most important signal for PF eligibility determination.

## VetsBoats PF Status Analysis

### Why VetsBoats is a Private Foundation

VetsBoats (EIN 464194065) files IRS Form 990-PF as a private foundation. Analysis of their filing history reveals why:

| Tax Year | Revenue | Contributions | Total Assets | Operating Fdn? | Grants to Orgs? |
|----------|---------|--------------|--------------|-----------------|------------------|
| 2023 | $278,676 | $278,676 | $352,566 | FALSE | FALSE |
| 2022 | $389,352 | $389,352 | $94,449 | FALSE | FALSE |
| 2020 | $52,594 | $52,594 | $125,092 | FALSE | FALSE |
| 2019 | $66,714 | $66,713 | $113,668 | FALSE | FALSE |
| 2018 | $7,750 | $7,750 | $69,380 | TRUE | FALSE |

**Key observations:**

1. **100% contribution-funded.** Revenue equals contributions received in every year. Zero program service revenue, zero investment income. This is a program-operating organization funded entirely by donors.

2. **Founder-funded origin.** Started with just $7,750 in 2018, consistent with a single founder writing a check. Organizations that start this small almost always fail the public support test because one or two donors make up the majority of lifetime support.

3. **Operating foundation flip.** Filed as a private operating foundation in 2018 (TRUE) but switched to non-operating (FALSE) from 2019 onward. This suggests they initially intended to qualify as an operating foundation (which gets slightly better treatment than a non-operating PF) but could not meet the income test or assets test requirements.

4. **Does not make grants.** `grants_to_organizations_ind = FALSE` in every year. VetsBoats runs programs directly (adaptive sailing for veterans) rather than making grants. This is consistent with a private operating foundation structure.

5. **Growth trajectory.** Revenue grew from $7.7K (2018) to $389K (2022) but pulled back to $279K (2023). The $352K in 2023 assets suggests healthy reserves, but the organization is still small by foundation standards.

### The PF Status Problem

VetsBoats' private foundation status creates an 83% eligibility barrier in the foundation grantmaker market. Of 53 foundations analyzed across 3 deep crawl batches:

- 32 (60%) were NOT_A_FIT, predominantly due to PF exclusion
- Only 9 (17%) were actionable (STRONG_FIT or POSSIBLE_FIT)
- Most foundations either explicitly require 509(a)(1)/(a)(2) public charity status or implicitly exclude PFs through "501(c)(3)" shorthand

### Known Foundation Funders

VetsBoats currently receives grants from 3 private foundations in our DB:

| Foundation | Amount | Year | Notes |
|-----------|--------|------|-------|
| Tahoe Maritime Foundation | $25,000 | 2023 | Known funder |
| Henry Mayo Newhall Foundation | $15,000 | 2023 | Known funder |
| Sidney Stern Memorial Trust | $3,500 | 2023 | Known funder |

Two additional known funders (Charis Fund, Bonnell Cove Foundation) are in the client profile but did not appear in the latest year's grant data. Total documented PF funding: $43,500 in 2023 (16% of revenue).

### The Reclassification Question

Under IRC 509(a)(1), an organization qualifies as a public charity if it receives at least one-third of its support from the general public. Critically, **grants from private foundations do NOT count toward this test**, but **grants from public charities DO count.** This means:

- The $43.5K from PF funders is useful revenue but does not help VetsBoats reclassify
- Grants from community foundations, federated campaigns, or government agencies would count toward public support
- Individual donations from the general public count (not just a few major donors)
- VetsBoats would need to demonstrate broad public support, not concentrated donor funding

If VetsBoats is using TheGrantScout to diversify its funding base, the most strategically valuable leads are not necessarily the largest grants, but grants from **public charities** that simultaneously provide revenue AND count toward the public support test.

1. **VetsBoats' PF status is a devastating eligibility barrier.** Of 53 foundations analyzed, 32 (60%) were NOT_A_FIT. Funding Judd Goldman Adaptive Sailing, Warrior Sailing, or Wounded Warrior is a much stronger signal than "has veteran grants in CA."

### Technical / Process

4. **Pre-filtering works.** The pre-filter script improved the actionable rate from 16% to 25%. Checking `app_restrictions` text for PF exclusion keywords, geographic restrictions, and mission mismatches before crawling is a no-brainer for future batches. The 5 HARD_FAIL eliminations were all correct.

5. **The "has funded PFs" signal needs cross-referencing.** Many DB records show PF recipients that are actually public charities with "Foundation" in their name, employee matching gifts, or DAF pass-throughs. Joining `fact_grants.recipient_ein` against `pf_returns.ein` to confirm actual 990-PF filers fixed the 65% false-positive rate. This should be standard for all future queries.

6. **Corporate foundations are a dead end for PF clients.** All 6 corporate foundations across batches 2-3 (Dick's, Bank of America, Cigna, TI, First Interstate, Liberty Mutual) were NOT_A_FIT. Corporate CSR programs almost universally require public charity status. Pre-filter them out for PF clients.

7. **"501(c)(3)" is ambiguous but context tells you the answer.** Large institutional funders ($100M+) saying "501(c)(3)" mean "public charity." Small family foundations saying "501(c)(3)" may genuinely include PFs. The legal distinction that matters is IRC 170(c) (includes PFs) vs 509(a) (excludes PFs).
