## Executive Summary

We scraped the websites of the top 15 foundations from the hard-filtered shortlist (340 foundations that passed all 3 data filters: open to applicants, gives in CA, has PF-to-PF grants) and assessed each for VetsBoats eligibility.

**Result: 14 of 15 are definitive NO. 1 is a MAYBE.**

The single prospect is the **Foundation for Global Sports Development** (CA-based, $1.4M/year), which has direct precedent funding both adaptive water sports and veteran therapeutic recreation. Every other foundation failed on multiple independent criteria, most commonly: private foundation exclusion, invitation-only grantmaking, and total mission misalignment.

**Key insight:** Data filters alone cannot identify viable prospects for a private foundation client. The three hard filters (open to applicants, CA geography, PF-to-PF grants) produced 340 foundations, but website verification eliminated 14 of the top 15. The "PF-to-PF grants" filter detects incidental grants to affiliated foundations (e.g., MacArthur granting to Driehaus to administer a MacArthur program), not genuine openness to funding external private foundations.

## Common Disqualifiers

### Why the Top 15 All Failed (Except GSD)

**Disqualifier 1: Private Foundation Exclusion (11 of 14 NOs)**

Most large foundations either explicitly exclude private foundations from eligibility (Hilton: "Not eligible: private foundations") or have never funded one outside of strategic intermediary relationships. VetsBoats' PF status is the single largest barrier.

The "PF-to-PF grants" data filter detected grants like:
- MacArthur granting $2.5M to Driehaus Foundation to administer a MacArthur arts program
- Ford granting to Rockefeller Brothers Fund for collaborative climate work
- Kresge granting to Lincoln Institute of Land Policy (an operating foundation)

These are NOT foundations open to funding external PFs. They are peer-foundation collaborations.

**Disqualifier 2: No Unsolicited Proposals (12 of 14 NOs)**

The vast majority of large foundations do not accept unsolicited applications. They identify grantees through program staff. "Open to applicants" in 990-PF data (`only_contri_to_preselected_ind = FALSE`) does NOT mean they accept cold applications. It means they don't exclusively fund board-selected grantees. The difference is critical.

**Disqualifier 3: Mission Misalignment (14 of 14 NOs)**

Every foundation except GSD has program areas with zero overlap to adaptive sailing for veterans:
- Systemic equity/racial justice: Kresge, CA Wellness, Weingart, Ford
- Journalism/democracy: Knight, Carnegie, MacArthur
- Children/education: Kellogg, Sobrato, Hewlett
- Regional focus: Heinz (PA), McKnight (MN), Dominion (utility territory)
- Invitation-only with narrow programs: Hilton (foster youth, refugees, Catholic sisters)

**Disqualifier 4: Geographic Restriction (5 of 14 NOs)**

Several foundations fund almost exclusively in their home region:
- Heinz: Southwestern Pennsylvania
- McKnight: Minnesota
- Sobrato: Silicon Valley specifically
- Weingart: 5 SoCal counties
- Dominion: VA/SC/NC/CT/RI/WV utility service areas

## Lessons for the Scoring Model

### What We Learned

1. **"PF-to-PF grants" is a misleading filter.** It detects foundation-to-foundation transfers for collaborative programs, not willingness to fund external PF applicants. The only foundation where this genuinely predicted PF funding was GSD (small, sports-focused, community-oriented).

2. **"Open to applicants" in 990 data is unreliable.** All 15 foundations had `only_contri_to_preselected_ind = FALSE`, yet 12 of 15 do not accept unsolicited proposals. The field captures a legal/IRS distinction, not operational grantmaking practice.

3. **Large foundations are systematically poor prospects for small PFs.** Foundations with $1B+ assets operate as strategic, invitation-driven funders with narrow program areas. Their "PF grants" are peer collaborations, not open funding. Small and mid-size foundations ($5M-$100M) are more likely to be genuine prospects.

4. **Mission keyword matches in grant purpose text are noisy.** Carnegie's "military" grants were all about nuclear weapons policy. Heinz's "veteran" grants were about Pittsburgh workforce development. Ford's were about journalism careers. The words "veteran" and "military" in grant purposes rarely mean "veteran services."

5. **Website verification is essential and cannot be skipped.** Every single data-qualified foundation failed website verification except GSD. A 1-in-15 hit rate means the data pipeline is necessary but not sufficient.

### Recommendations for Future Pipeline

1. **Add a "foundation size" filter.** Foundations under $100M assets are more likely to fund individual programs like VetsBoats. Above $1B, the hit rate approaches zero.

2. **Weight mission-specific keywords more heavily.** "Adaptive," "sailing," "therapeutic recreation," "outdoor recreation" in grant purposes are much more predictive than "veteran" or "military."

3. **Add a "accepts unsolicited applications" web-verified field.** This can't be determined from 990 data. Consider scraping foundation websites at scale for application page presence.

4. **For PF clients specifically, filter for foundations with a pattern of small-dollar PF grants** ($5K-$50K range), not just any PF-to-PF transfer.

5. **Consider the "operating foundation treated as public charity" angle.** VetsBoats runs programs directly (adaptive sailing), which is the hallmark of an operating foundation. If VetsBoats were reclassified as a private operating foundation under IRS Section 4942(j)(3), some foundations' PF exclusions might not apply.

## Notes

1. **The 93% false-positive rate is specific to private foundation clients.** For public charity clients (501(c)(3)), the data filters would perform much better because the PF exclusion issue doesn't apply. This finding is VetsBoats-specific, not a general product problem.

2. **GSD's declining assets ($24M to $7M in 4 years) suggest urgency.** If they're in spend-down mode, outreach sooner is better.

3. **Heinz Endowments has a real veterans program ($7.6M)** but is PA-only and requires public charity status. If VetsBoats ever converts to 501(c)(3), Heinz would be a strong prospect.

4. **The "expenditure responsibility" mechanism** allows any private foundation to grant to another PF under IRS rules. The legal barrier isn't absolute, but it adds compliance burden that most foundations avoid. Only GSD showed a pattern of accepting this burden for mission-aligned organizations.

5. **15 parallel web scraping agents completed in ~5 minutes.** This approach could be scaled to verify the full 340-foundation list, but at significant computational cost. A tiered approach (verify top 50 first) would be more efficient.
