## Executive Summary

Two review rounds (6-perspective expert review + 4-persona stress test) identified systemic failures in 20 cold emails generated by Claude Code. The problems trace back to five root causes in the prompting:

1. **The foundation selection table was sorted by total_giving_5yr DESC with no guardrails.** Claude picked from the top of the list, which meant the biggest (and most inappropriate) foundations dominated every email.
2. **The prompt said "vary the wording" but prescribed a single structure.** Claude interpreted "variation" as lexical substitution within an identical skeleton, producing 18/20 emails with the same [federal funding concern] + [two foundations] + [free list offer] pattern.
3. **The prompt banned company identification ("Do NOT mention TheGrantScout") without providing an alternative identity frame.** This created an evasion problem: no company name, no title, no address -- which reads as deceptive, not casual.
4. **The prompt said "use mission_description to inform foundation picks" but the cohort_foundation_lists table had no mission-level data.** The table only contained state + NTEE sector + total giving. There was no mechanism for Claude to match foundations to a nonprofit's specific niche.
5. **There was no review step in the generation pipeline.** The prompt said "draft 20 emails" and the output was the final product. No scoring, no fact-checking, no rejection criteria.

This report redesigns every component of the email generation prompt. Section 10 contains the complete, ready-to-use master prompt.

## 1. Root Cause Analysis

### Problem A: Foundation Selection (40% of emails cited inappropriate mega-foundations)

**Original prompt language:**
> "Use the prospect's mission_description to inform which foundations you pick from the cohort list (pick the 2-3 most relevant to their specific work, not just the biggest)"

**Original SQL (build_cohorts_v2.sql, line 41):**
> `ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY fss.state, fss.sector ORDER BY fss.total_giving_5yr DESC) as rk`

**Why it failed:** The prompt told Claude to pick the "most relevant" foundations, but the only data Claude had was a table of 15 foundations per state+sector combo, ranked by total giving. The top entries were always mega-foundations (Ford, Mellon, Lilly, Packard, Hewlett). Claude had no relevance signals beyond state+sector, so "most relevant" defaulted to "highest ranked" -- i.e., biggest. The prompt instruction was aspirational but the data infrastructure made it impossible to follow.

The example grant per foundation was also chosen by `ORDER BY fg.amount DESC` (build_cohorts_v2.sql, line 60), so the example was always the largest grant, not the most relevant one. This produced mismatches like Helpmate Inc (DV org) cited as "similar work" for a disability center, because Helpmate received the largest grant in the NC human services category from that foundation.

**Additional failure:** Lilly Endowment appeared in the NY and CT cohort lists because it made some grants to national organizations headquartered in those states. The SQL matched on `recipient_state`, which reflects the recipient's filing state, not the foundation's geographic focus. A national org like National Urban League files in NY, so Lilly's grant to them shows up under "NY Human Services" -- but Lilly gives 95% of its money in Indiana.

### Problem B: Template Visibility (18/20 identical structure)

**Original prompt language:**
> "The opening line about federal funding can be worded differently each time"
> "The transition to foundation names can vary"
> "The closing offer can vary"
> "Vary the wording naturally so no two emails read the same"

**Why it failed:** All four variation instructions are about lexical substitution within the same structure. The prompt prescribed a rigid 4-part structure (Why + Value + Offer + Sign-off) with "federal funding" as the mandatory opening hook and "top X list" as the mandatory offer. Claude correctly interpreted "vary" as "paraphrase the same idea in different words." The result: 18/20 emails open with a federal funding concern sentence, all 20 name two foundations with dollar amounts, all 20 close with a list offer. The variation was cosmetic.

The prompt also provided a single example email, which anchored Claude's output heavily. One example = one template. Claude reproduced the example's cadence, structure, and even specific phrases ("worth a look," "caught my eye," "worth knowing") because those patterns were embedded in or adjacent to the example.

### Problem C: Missing Identity (feels evasive, CAN-SPAM non-compliant)

**Original prompt language:**
> "Do NOT mention TheGrantScout, 990 data, analyzing anything, or our product/service"
> "Sign off with just 'Alec' (no company name, no title -- keep it personal)"

**Why it failed:** The prompt confused "personal" with "anonymous." A personal email from a colleague still has context: you know who they are and why they're reaching out. An anonymous email from a stranger who withholds their company name, title, and the reason they have this data is not personal -- it is suspicious. The ethics reviewer called this "deceptive by omission" and the ED persona said it "feels evasive, not casual." The prompt also made the emails CAN-SPAM non-compliant (missing: commercial message identification, physical address, unsubscribe mechanism).

### Problem D: Accuracy Failures (Lilly for CT, Helpmate for disability center, Andrea/Sue mismatch)

**Original prompt language:**
> "Use the prospect's mission_description to inform which foundations you pick"

**Why it failed:** This was a soft instruction with no enforcement mechanism. The prompt did not:
- Tell Claude to verify that the foundation's geographic focus matches the prospect's state
- Tell Claude to verify that the example grant recipient is doing similar work to the prospect
- Tell Claude to verify that the contact name matches the email address
- Provide any data about the foundation's geographic giving concentration
- Provide any data about what the example grant recipient actually does

Claude had no way to know that Lilly Endowment is 95% Indiana-focused, because the cohort table only showed raw giving amounts by recipient state. Claude had no way to know that Helpmate Inc does domestic violence work, because the table only showed recipient name and grant amount.

The Andrea/Sue mismatch (Email 20) is a data quality problem upstream of the prompt: the contact_first_name field was extracted from all_officers JSONB, but the email address belongs to a different person. The prompt should have included a verification step but did not.

### Problem E: No Quality Gate (20 emails generated and marked ready to send)

**Original prompt language:**
> "Draft them yourself directly as a Claude Code agent"
> "Save ALL output files..."

**Why it failed:** The prompt treated email generation as a single pass with no review. The email prompt engineering research (PROMPT_2026-02-16_email_prompt_engineering.md) explicitly called for a "review prompt" and "scoring rubric" and "AI-as-reviewer" pass, but these were never implemented before the 20 emails were drafted. The fix-prep-draft prompt went straight from database prep to email generation to output files.

## 2. Foundation Selection Prompt Redesign

### What the Data Table Needs

The current `cohort_foundation_lists` table is missing critical fields. Before any email generation, the table must be rebuilt with these additions:

**New columns needed in cohort_foundation_lists:**

| Column | Type | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| `foundation_state` | VARCHAR(2) | Foundation's home state |
| `geographic_concentration` | NUMERIC | % of grants going to this recipient state |
| `total_assets` | NUMERIC | Foundation endowment size |
| `median_grant_in_state` | NUMERIC | Median grant to recipients in THIS state (not overall) |
| `unique_recipients_in_state` | INTEGER | Number of distinct recipients in this state |
| `example_recipient_ntee` | VARCHAR(10) | NTEE code of the example recipient |
| `example_recipient_mission` | TEXT | Mission description of the example recipient |
| `invitation_only` | BOOLEAN | Whether foundation is known to be invitation-only |
| `national_funder` | BOOLEAN | Whether >50% of grants go to 5+ states |

**New SQL ranking logic:**

Replace `ORDER BY total_giving_5yr DESC` with a composite score:

```sql
ORDER BY (
    0.4 * (geographic_concentration)  -- geographic focus on this state
  + 0.3 * (1.0 / NULLIF(LOG(total_assets + 1), 0))  -- penalty for mega-size
  + 0.2 * (unique_recipients_in_state::float / NULLIF(unique_recipients_total, 0))  -- state commitment
  + 0.1 * (CASE WHEN total_giving_5yr > 100000 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)  -- minimum activity threshold
) DESC
```

This deprioritizes mega-foundations (inverse log of assets) and rewards foundations with high geographic concentration in the prospect's state.

**Banned foundations list:**

Create a reference table `f990_2025.ref_foundation_blacklist`:

| Foundation | EIN | Reason |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Ford Foundation | 131684331 | Invitation-only, $16B, strategic initiatives |
| Andrew W. Mellon Foundation | 131879954 | Mostly invitation-only, $9B |
| Lilly Endowment | 350868122 | 95% Indiana-only, misleading when cited for other states |
| Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation | 562618866 | Invitation-only, $70B |
| David and Lucile Packard Foundation | 942278431 | Strategic grantmaking, $10B |
| William and Flora Hewlett Foundation | 941655673 | Strategic grantmaking, $11B |
| Robert W. Woodruff Foundation | 581695425 | Relationship-based, $8B |
| MacArthur Foundation | 237093598 | Strategic initiatives, $8B |
| Edna McConnell Clark Foundation | 131680355 | Now Blue Meridian Partners, invitation-only |

This list should be excluded from cohort_foundation_lists entirely, not just flagged.

### New Prompt Language for Foundation Selection

**REPLACE** the original:
> "pick the 2-3 most relevant to their specific work, not just the biggest"

**WITH:**

```
FOUNDATION SELECTION RULES (CRITICAL -- violations will make the email worse than not sending it):

For each prospect, you will receive a ranked list of foundations from cohort_foundation_lists.
Select exactly 2 foundations per email. Apply these filters IN ORDER:

1. GEOGRAPHIC FIT: The foundation must have geographic_concentration >= 15% for the
   prospect's state, OR the foundation must be headquartered in the prospect's state.
   If a foundation gives 95% of its money in Indiana, do not cite it for a New York
   nonprofit, even if it made one grant there.

2. SCALE FIT: The foundation's median_grant_in_state must be within 0.2x to 10x of the
   prospect's total_revenue. A $500K-grant foundation should not be cited for a $200K
   nonprofit. A $5M-grant foundation should not be cited for a $600K nonprofit. Match
   the scale.

3. NICHE FIT: Read the prospect's mission_description. Read the example_recipient_mission
   for each candidate foundation. The example recipient must do recognizably similar work
   to the prospect. If the prospect does disability services and the example recipient does
   domestic violence work, that foundation is not a match -- even if both are "human
   services."

4. DISCOVERY VALUE: Do not cite the most obvious or famous foundation in the state. If every
   grant writer in that state already knows about it, it does not demonstrate your value.
   Prefer foundations ranked 3-10 on the list over foundations ranked 1-2. The first
   foundation you cite should make the recipient think "I haven't heard of that one" -- not
   "everyone knows about them."

5. NEVER CITE: Any foundation with total_assets > $5 billion. Any foundation on the
   ref_foundation_blacklist. Any foundation where invitation_only = true. Any foundation
   where the example grant recipient is not recognizably similar to the prospect.

For the example grant, choose one where the recipient does similar work to the prospect.
If the best available example is not a good match, omit the example recipient name and
just cite the dollar amount: "with grants typically around $75,000 for [sector] organizations."
Do NOT cite an unrelated organization as doing "similar work."
```

**Why this fixes it:** The original failure was structural (data sorted by size with no relevance signals). This new prompt adds five explicit filters with concrete thresholds. It bans mega-foundations by asset size, requires geographic concentration data, requires niche matching via mission descriptions, and explicitly tells Claude to prefer less-obvious foundations. The instruction to check example recipient similarity prevents the Helpmate/disability mismatch.

## 3. Opening Line Prompt Redesign

### The Problem

**Original prompt language:**
> "The opening line about federal funding can be worded differently each time -- don't use the exact same sentence 20 times"

**What happened:** 18/20 emails opened with a federal funding concern sentence. The prompt mandated this hook and only asked for word-level variation. Claude delivered exactly what was asked: 18 paraphrases of the same idea.

### 7 Distinct Opening Strategies

The prompt must provide multiple structurally different opening strategies, not just word variations of one hook.

```
OPENING LINE STRATEGIES -- Use each strategy AT LEAST ONCE in a batch of 20.
NEVER use the same strategy more than 4 times in a batch of 20.
Assign strategies based on the prospect's situation, not randomly.

STRATEGY 1: MISSION ACKNOWLEDGMENT (use for: orgs with distinctive missions)
Name what the org does in one specific sentence. No generic "your important work."
Example: "Centro de Apoyo Familiar is one of a few organizations providing bilingual
family support services in the Merrimack Valley."
When to use: When mission_description reveals a specific, describable niche.

STRATEGY 2: SECTOR SHIFT (use for: orgs likely dependent on government funding)
Reference a behavioral shift in their sector, not a crisis.
Example: "A lot of behavioral health organizations in Washington are exploring
foundation funding for the first time."
When to use: When the org's sector has high government funding dependency (health,
housing, social services) and the org has few existing foundation grants.

STRATEGY 3: GEOGRAPHIC DISCOVERY (use for: orgs in states with underserved foundation landscapes)
Note something specific about the foundation landscape in their area.
Example: "Maine has a smaller foundation community than most states, but there are
207 that actively fund human services there."
When to use: When the state has fewer than 200 foundations in the cohort.

STRATEGY 4: PEER SIGNAL (use for: orgs whose peers have received notable grants)
Reference a peer organization that received a notable grant.
Example: "Heading Home Inc just received $1 million from Liberty Mutual for family
shelter work in Massachusetts."
When to use: When the cohort_foundation_lists has a strong example grant to a
genuinely comparable organization.

STRATEGY 5: FOUNDATION ENTRY (use for: orgs in sectors where new funders are entering)
Name a foundation that recently started giving in their space.
Example: "The Satterberg Foundation started funding domestic violence organizations
in Washington in 2022."
When to use: When a foundation in the cohort list has a recent giving_trend of
'growing' and a short history in that state+sector.

STRATEGY 6: DIRECT RESEARCH STATEMENT (use for: any org, especially when other strategies don't fit)
State plainly what you've been doing and why.
Example: "We've been researching which private foundations fund disability services
in North Carolina."
When to use: As a fallback when none of the other strategies naturally fit the org.
This is the most honest and least clever opening -- which is sometimes the best approach.

STRATEGY 7: QUESTION OPENER (use for: orgs in sectors with low foundation awareness)
Ask a genuine question about their funding approach.
Example: "Has your team explored private foundation funding for workforce development?"
When to use: When the org appears to be primarily government-funded and may not
have considered foundation grants. Do NOT use if the org already has foundation
relationships.

RULE: Track which strategy you used for each email. Before writing email N, review
the strategies used for emails 1 through N-1. If any strategy has been used 4 times,
it is locked out for the rest of the batch. This is a hard ceiling, not a guideline.

NEVER open with:
- Generic crisis language ("it's been a tough year," "these days," "after everything happening")
- Pain-point framing ("struggling with," "feeling pressure from," "navigating uncertainty")
- Any sentence that could apply to every nonprofit in every state in every sector
```

**Why this fixes it:** The original prompt provided one hook (federal funding) with permission to vary the words. This provides seven structurally different strategies with explicit assignment rules (when to use each based on org characteristics) and a hard ceiling on repetition. The strategies range from mission-specific to geographic to peer-based, ensuring that even if Claude defaults to repetition, the ceiling forces at least 4 different openings in a 20-email batch.

## 4. Anti-Template Instructions

### The Problem

**Original prompt language:**
> "vary the wording naturally so no two emails read the same"

**What happened:** Every email had the same skeleton: [1 sentence about funding uncertainty] + [2-3 sentences with foundation names and dollar amounts] + [1 sentence offering the list] + [Alec]. Claude varied words within this skeleton but never varied the skeleton itself.

### New Prompt Language

```
STRUCTURAL VARIATION RULES:

An email batch must not have a visible template. If a recipient knows someone who
received a different email from the same batch and they compare the two, the emails
should look like they were written separately -- not generated from a template with
different merge fields.

ELEMENTS THAT MUST VARY ACROSS EMAILS (not just in word choice, but in structure):

1. PARAGRAPH COUNT: Some emails should be 2 paragraphs. Some should be 3. Some
   should be 4 short lines. Do not use the same paragraph count for more than
   40% of the batch.

2. OPENING STRATEGY: See Opening Line Strategies above. Minimum 4 different
   strategies per 20-email batch.

3. FOUNDATION PRESENTATION FORMAT: Rotate between these formats:
   - Format A: "[Foundation] has given $X to [sector] in [state], with a typical
     grant around $Y."
   - Format B: "[Foundation] recently gave $Y to [comparable org] for [purpose]."
   - Format C: "[Foundation] funds [sector] organizations in [state] -- their
     typical grant is around $Y."
   - Format D: "The [Foundation] has been funding [sector] work in [state] since
     [year], most recently giving $Y to [comparable org]."
   Use each format at least twice per 20-email batch. Never use the same format
   for both foundations in the same email.

4. OFFER FRAMING: Rotate between these (see Section 8 for details):
   - "Want me to send it?"
   - "Worth a look?"
   - "Interested?"
   - "Just reply and I'll send it over."
   - Omit the explicit ask entirely and let the information speak for itself.

5. FOUNDATION PLACEMENT: In some emails, lead with the foundation names (value
   first, context second). In others, provide context first and foundations second.
   Do not always put foundations in the middle.

6. TRANSITION PHRASES: The following phrases may each be used AT MOST ONCE in a
   20-email batch: "worth a look," "caught my eye," "stood out," "worth knowing,"
   "you might not know," "on your radar." Once used, they are locked out.
   Preferably, do not use advisory phrases at all. Simply state the data.

BANNED STRUCTURAL PATTERN:
Do not write more than 3 emails in a batch that follow this exact skeleton:
[funding concern sentence] + [transition phrase] + [Foundation 1 stat] +
[Foundation 2 stat] + [list offer sentence] + [sign-off].
This was the pattern in 18/20 emails in batch 1, and it made the template
immediately visible.

SELF-CHECK: After drafting all emails in a batch, read them back-to-back.
If you can identify the template by email 3, rewrite emails 4-20 with more
structural variation.
```

**Why this fixes it:** The original "vary the wording" instruction was about surface-level changes. This prompt specifies six dimensions of structural variation (paragraph count, opening strategy, foundation presentation format, offer framing, foundation placement, transition phrases), sets hard limits on repetition per dimension, and explicitly bans the skeleton that dominated batch 1. The self-check instruction creates a meta-awareness step.

## 5. Tone and Phrasing Rules

### The Problem

**Original prompt language:**
> "like texting a colleague a useful resource"
> "Do NOT say 'I analyze' or 'I study' or 'I track'"

**What happened:** The banned list was too narrow (3 phrases) and the tone calibration was too casual ("texting a colleague"). The result was emails that were casual enough to feel breezy but not professional enough to feel credible. Specific failure phrases included "for free" (suspicion trigger), "to help" (presumptuous), "worth a look" / "you should know about" (condescending), and "Happy to send it over" (too casual for a stranger).

### Complete Banned Phrases List

```
BANNED PHRASES AND REPLACEMENTS:

PRESUMPTUOUS (assumes a relationship or need that doesn't exist):
- "to help" / "to help you" --> delete entirely, or use "in case it's useful"
- "you should know about" --> "that stood out in the data" (use max 1x per batch)
- "you might not know" --> delete entirely (if they already know it, you look foolish)
- "worth a look" --> delete, or "that fund [their specific work]" (max 1x per batch)
- "worth knowing" --> same as above
- "caught my eye" --> delete (simulates personal discovery, feels fake at scale)
- "funders worth a look" --> delete (positions you as their advisor when you're a stranger)
- "you might not have on your radar" --> delete

SUSPICION TRIGGERS:
- "for free" --> delete entirely. Just offer the thing. Saying "free" implies a paid version exists.
- "free list" --> "I put together [number] foundations..." (the offer is implicit)
- "leads" (in subject lines or body) --> "funders" or "foundations"
- "list" (in subject lines) --> drop it. "Massachusetts housing funders" not "Massachusetts housing funders list"

TOO CASUAL FOR A STRANGER:
- "Happy to send it over" --> "Want me to send it?" or "Interested?"
- "Want me to send it along?" --> "Want me to send it?"
- "Just checking in" --> never use in follow-ups
- "Hey [Name]" --> "Hi [Name]" (hey is too familiar for first contact)

FILLER AND CLICHE:
- "These days" --> delete the sentence
- "It's been a tough year" --> delete (most cliched phrase in nonprofit communications)
- "After everything happening with..." --> delete (vague hand-waving)
- "The past year has been tough" --> delete
- "In today's challenging landscape" --> delete
- "navigating" (funding, uncertainty, etc.) --> delete the sentence

AI VOICE TELLS:
- "I came across your organization" --> never use
- "I was impressed by" --> never use
- Starting with "I" --> rephrase so the subject is the org, sector, or data
- Three adjectives in a row --> use one
- Generic mission paraphrasing --> use one specific detail, not a summary
- "I wanted to reach out because" --> delete, just state the reason

SALES LANGUAGE:
- "leads" --> "funders" or "foundations"
- "prospects" --> never use in prospect-facing communication
- "top 200 list" --> see Section 8 for replacement
- "We compiled" / "We put together" / "We pulled together" --> "I found" or "I've been researching"

CONDESCENDING FRAMING:
- Any sentence that implies the recipient doesn't know something obvious
  ("You might not know about [famous foundation]")
- Any sentence that implies the recipient needs saving ("to help organizations like yours")
- Any sentence that positions the sender as an expert advising a novice

TONE CALIBRATION:
The correct tone is NOT "texting a colleague" (too casual).
The correct tone is: a peer in your sector who has access to data you might find
useful, sharing it in a short, straightforward email. Professional but not formal.
Specific but not showy. Like an email from someone you met at a conference who
followed up with something relevant.

Think: "I ran across some data that might be relevant to your work at [org]."
Not: "Hey! Check out these foundations!"
Not: "Dear Ms. Rodriguez, I am writing to inform you..."
```

**Why this fixes it:** The original banned list had 3 entries. This has 30+, organized by failure mode (presumptuous, suspicious, too casual, filler, AI tells, sales, condescending). Each ban includes a rationale so Claude understands WHY the phrase fails, not just that it is forbidden. The tone calibration replaces the vague "texting a colleague" with a concrete analogy: "someone you met at a conference who followed up with something relevant."

## 6. Accuracy Guard Rails

### The Problem

**Original prompt language:**
> "Use the prospect's mission_description to inform which foundations you pick"

**What happened:**
- Lilly Endowment cited for NY and CT nonprofits (it gives 95% in Indiana)
- Helpmate Inc (DV org) cited as "similar work" for a disability center
- Ford Foundation cited for a motor racing archive
- Email 20 greets "Andrea" but sends to "sue@respectivesolutions.com"
- "Top 200" claimed for states with fewer than 200 relevant foundations

### New Prompt Language

```
ACCURACY RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE -- any violation means the email must not be sent):

RULE 1: GEOGRAPHIC VERIFICATION
Before citing a foundation, check its foundation_state and geographic_concentration.
If the foundation is headquartered in a different state AND geographic_concentration
for the prospect's state is below 15%, do NOT cite it. The foundation may have made
a few grants in that state to national organizations, but it is not a funder of
local organizations in that state.

Specific known geographic traps:
- Lilly Endowment (EIN 350868122): 95% Indiana. Never cite for any other state.
- Duke Endowment: Primarily Carolinas. Do not cite for other Southern states.
- Houston Endowment: Primarily Houston metro. Do not cite for all of Texas.
- Kresge Foundation: National but strategic. Do not cite as a state-specific funder.

RULE 2: SECTOR/NICHE VERIFICATION
Before citing an example grant recipient as doing "similar work" to the prospect:
- Read the prospect's mission_description
- Read the example_recipient_mission (or infer from name and NTEE code)
- They must serve the same population OR do the same type of work
- "Human services" is NOT specific enough. Domestic violence services, disability
  services, refugee services, youth development, and senior services are all "human
  services" but they are NOT similar work.
- If in doubt, omit the example recipient name entirely and say "with grants
  typically around $X to [sector] organizations in [state]."

RULE 3: SCALE VERIFICATION
- Do not cite a foundation's national giving total for a state-specific email.
  If Lilly gave $109M nationally but only $200K in New York, cite $200K (or don't
  cite Lilly at all -- $200K from a $24B endowment suggests this state is not a
  priority).
- Dollar amounts cited must be the foundation's giving in the prospect's state
  and sector, not their overall giving.
- Grant sizes cited must be plausible for the prospect's budget. If the prospect's
  total revenue is $400K, citing a "$5 million grant to [org]" does not
  demonstrate relevance -- it demonstrates scale mismatch.

RULE 4: CONTACT NAME VERIFICATION
Before writing "Hi [Name]":
- Verify that contact_first_name plausibly matches the email address
- If the email is sue@domain.com but the contact name is "Andrea," FLAG this
  email for manual review. Do not send it. The officer data and the email address
  belong to different people.
- If the contact_first_name is derived from an officer whose title does not
  suggest they handle fundraising (e.g., "Treasurer" or "Secretary"), note this
  as a risk. The email may reach the right inbox but greet the wrong person.

RULE 5: CLAIM SOURCING
Every factual claim in the email must trace to a specific field in the prospect's
profile or the cohort_foundation_lists data. The generator may NOT:
- Round numbers (if the data says $4,173,422, say "nearly $4.2 million" not
  "$4 million" or "$5 million")
- Infer details not in the data (do not say "growing their giving" unless the
  giving_trend field says "growing")
- Add context about the foundation beyond what's in the data (do not describe
  what the foundation "focuses on" unless the data includes that information)
- Invent purpose descriptions for grants when purpose_text is null

RULE 6: LIST COUNT ACCURACY
- If the cohort has 200+ foundations, say "over 200 foundations" (not "top 200")
- If the cohort has fewer than 200, use the exact number (67, 111, 137)
- NEVER claim a round number (200, 100, 50) when the real number is different
- The specific, non-round number is always more credible than the round one
```

### Pre-Generation Data Quality Checks

```
BEFORE generating any emails, run these checks on the batch data:

CHECK 1: For each prospect, verify contact_first_name appears in the email address
or officer list. Flag mismatches.

CHECK 2: For each foundation in cohort_foundation_lists, verify it is not on
ref_foundation_blacklist.

CHECK 3: For each prospect, verify the subject line sector matches the body sector.
(Email 16 had "children's services" in subject but "human services" in body.)

CHECK 4: For each foundation cited, verify geographic_concentration >= 15% for
the prospect's state.

CHECK 5: For each example grant recipient, verify the recipient's NTEE first letter
matches the prospect's NTEE first letter. If not, omit the recipient name.

Output a data quality report before generating any emails. Any prospect that fails
CHECK 1 goes to a manual review queue, not the email batch.
```

**Why this fixes it:** The original prompt had one soft instruction ("use mission_description to inform"). ## 8. The Offer Redesign

### The Problem

**Original prompt language:**
> "We have the top 200 list for free. Want a copy?"

**What happened:**
- "Top 200" sounds like a data dump (grant writers work 20-40 prospects per year)
- "For free" triggers suspicion ("what's the paid version?")
- "List" in subject lines triggers spam filters
- Round numbers (200) feel less credible than specific numbers (67, 111, 137)

### New Prompt Language

```
THE OFFER:

What we're offering: A curated set of foundations that fund organizations similar to
theirs, in their state and sector, with enough detail to be actionable (foundation
name, typical grant size, recent example grants, whether they accept applications).

WHAT TO CALL IT:
- "a breakdown of [number] foundations that fund [their sector] in [their state]"
- "[number] foundations that match your profile"
- "the full [number]-foundation research"
- NEVER call it a "list." "List" implies a raw data dump. Use "research," "breakdown,"
  "profile," or "analysis."

NUMBER RULES:
- Always use the real number from the cohort, even if it's large
- For cohorts with 200+ foundations, say "over 200" (never "top 200" -- "top" implies
  ranking by some metric the recipient doesn't understand)
- For cohorts under 200, use the exact number: "67 foundations," "111 foundations,"
  "137 foundations"
- Specific numbers are more credible than round ones. 67 feels counted. 200 feels made up.
- Emails 11 (137), 12 (111), and 13 (67) consistently outperformed emails with "200"
  in both review rounds.

"FREE" RULES:
- NEVER say "for free" or "free list" or "at no cost"
- Just offer it. "I put together a breakdown of 67 Kansas housing foundations.
  Want me to send it?" The absence of a price implies it's free without triggering
  the suspicion that comes with saying "free."

CTA OPTIONS (rotate, never use the same CTA more than 4x per 20-email batch):
- "Want me to send it?"
- "Interested?"
- "Worth a look?"
- "Just reply and I'll send it over."
- No CTA at all (let the information stand alone -- works for very strong emails)

NEVER USE:
- "Happy to send it over" (too breezy, no clear question mark, passive)
- "Let me know if it'd be useful" (creates decision fatigue -- they have to evaluate

- "Schedule a call to discuss" (violates the entire give-first approach)
```

**Why this fixes it:** The offer is renamed from "list" to "research/breakdown/profile." The fake-sounding "free" is dropped. The number is always real, never rounded. The CTAs are rotated with a hard limit. The passive/ambiguous CTAs ("Happy to send it over," "Let me know if it'd be useful") are banned with explanations.

```
# EMAIL GENERATION PROMPT: Nonprofit Cold Outreach (Give-First Approach)

## Context

You are drafting personalized cold emails to nonprofit prospects. Each email offers
a curated foundation research set -- a collection of private foundations that fund
organizations like theirs in their state and sector.

The emails should be genuinely useful standalone communications, not sales pitches.
The recipient should get value from reading the email even if they never reply.

## Input Data Per Prospect

For each prospect, you will receive:
- contact_first_name: First name of the contact
- contact_email: Email address
- org_name: Organization name
- state: Two-letter state code
- sector_label: Human-readable sector (e.g., "Human Services," "Housing")
- ntee_code: Full NTEE code
- mission_description: The org's mission statement
- total_revenue: Approximate annual revenue
- foundation_count: Number of foundations in their state+sector cohort
- cohort_foundation_data: Top 15 foundations with fields:
  - foundation_name, foundation_state, total_giving_5yr, median_grant_in_state,
    geographic_concentration, total_assets, unique_recipients_in_state,
    example_recipient_name, example_recipient_ntee, example_recipient_mission,
    example_grant_amount, invitation_only, national_funder

## Email Specifications

- LENGTH: 50-90 words in the body (before signature)
- FORMAT: Plain text. No links, no images, no HTML formatting, no bold, no bullets.
- PARAGRAPHS: 2-4 short paragraphs. Vary the count across the batch.
- SUBJECT LINE: 4-7 words. Reference their state or sector. No clickbait, no "list,"
  no "leads," no "free." Include a number when natural.
  Examples: "67 Kansas housing foundations" / "Washington behavioral health funders" /
  "Know about the Sunderland Foundation?"

## Foundation Selection Rules

For each email, select exactly 2 foundations from the prospect's cohort data.
Apply these filters IN ORDER -- a foundation must pass ALL filters:

1. GEOGRAPHIC FIT: geographic_concentration >= 15% for the prospect's state,
   OR the foundation is headquartered in the prospect's state.

2. SCALE FIT: median_grant_in_state is between 0.2x and 10x the prospect's
   total_revenue. A foundation giving $5M grants is not relevant to a $400K org.

3. NICHE FIT: Read the prospect's mission_description. Read the example_recipient_mission.
   The example recipient must do recognizably similar work. "Human services" is not
   specific enough -- disability services, domestic violence services, refugee services,
   and youth development are different niches.

4. DISCOVERY VALUE: Prefer foundations ranked 3-10 over foundations ranked 1-2.
   The first foundation cited should make the recipient think "I haven't heard of
   that one" -- not "everyone knows about them."

5. HARD EXCLUSIONS:
   - total_assets > $5 billion
   - invitation_only = true
   - Foundation is on ref_foundation_blacklist
   - Lilly Endowment for any state other than Indiana
   - Example recipient does different work than the prospect

For the example grant, cite the recipient only if they do similar work to the prospect.
If no good match exists, say "with grants typically around $X to [sector] organizations
in [state]" without naming a specific recipient.

## Opening Line Strategies

Use each strategy AT LEAST ONCE in a batch of 20. NEVER use any strategy more than
4 times. Assign based on the prospect's situation.

STRATEGY 1 -- MISSION ACKNOWLEDGMENT (for orgs with distinctive missions):
Name what the org does in one specific sentence. Use a detail from mission_description.
"Centro de Apoyo Familiar is one of a few organizations providing bilingual family
support services in the Merrimack Valley."

STRATEGY 2 -- SECTOR SHIFT (for government-funding-dependent orgs):
Reference a behavioral shift, not a crisis.
"A lot of behavioral health organizations in Washington are exploring foundation
funding for the first time."

STRATEGY 3 -- GEOGRAPHIC DISCOVERY (for states with <200 foundations in cohort):
Note something about the foundation landscape in their area.
"Maine has a smaller foundation community, but there are 207 that actively fund
human services there."

STRATEGY 4 -- PEER SIGNAL (when cohort has strong example grant to comparable org):
Reference a peer organization that received a notable grant.
"Heading Home Inc just received $1 million from Liberty Mutual for family shelter
work in Massachusetts."

STRATEGY 5 -- FOUNDATION ENTRY (when a cohort foundation has growing giving trend):
Name a foundation that recently started giving in their space.
"The Satterberg Foundation started funding domestic violence organizations in
Washington in 2022."

STRATEGY 6 -- DIRECT RESEARCH STATEMENT (fallback):
State plainly what you've been researching.
"I've been researching which private foundations fund disability services in
North Carolina."

STRATEGY 7 -- QUESTION OPENER (for orgs with low foundation awareness):
Ask a genuine question about their funding approach.
"Has your team explored private foundation funding for workforce development?"

NEVER open with:
- "It's been a tough year" / "These days" / "After everything happening"
- Any generic crisis/uncertainty language
- Any sentence that applies equally to every nonprofit in every state

Track strategy usage per email. Hard ceiling: 4 uses per strategy per batch.

## Foundation Presentation Formats

Rotate between these. Use each at least twice per batch. Never use the same format
for both foundations in one email.

Format A: "[Foundation] has given $X to [sector] in [state], with a typical grant
around $Y."

Format B: "[Foundation] recently gave $Y to [comparable org] for [purpose]."

Format C: "[Foundation] funds [sector] organizations in [state]. Their typical grant
is around $Y."

Format D: "The [Foundation] has been funding [sector] work in [state] since [year],
most recently giving $Y to [comparable org]."

## The Offer

What we're offering is a curated set of foundation research, not a raw data dump.

NAMING: Use "research," "breakdown," or "foundations" -- never "list."
- "a breakdown of [number] foundations that fund [sector] in [state]"
- "[number] foundations that match [org_name]'s profile"
- "the full [number]-foundation research"

NUMBERS:
- Cohorts with 200+: say "over 200"
- Cohorts under 200: use exact number (67, 111, 137)
- Specific non-round numbers are always more credible

"FREE" RULE: NEVER say "for free," "free list," or "at no cost." Just offer it.
The absence of a price implies it's free.

CTA ROTATION (max 4 uses each per batch of 20):
- "Want me to send it?"
- "Interested?"
- "Worth a look?"
- "Just reply and I'll send it over."
- No explicit CTA (let the information stand alone)

NEVER USE: "Happy to send it over" / "Let me know if it'd be useful"

## Signature Block

Every email ends with:

Alec Kleinman
TheGrantScout
[rotate one-liner: "Foundation funding research for nonprofits." /
"We research foundation giving patterns for nonprofits." /
"We help nonprofits find foundations that match their work."]

Reply STOP to opt out.

## Tone

The correct register: a peer in your sector who has access to data you might find
useful, sharing it in a short, straightforward email. Professional but not formal.
Specific but not showy. Like someone you met briefly at a conference who followed
up with something relevant.

Use "I" in the body (personal voice). "We" only in the signature line.

## Banned Phrases

PRESUMPTUOUS: "to help" / "you should know about" / "you might not know" /
"worth a look" (max 1x per batch) / "worth knowing" / "caught my eye" (max 1x) /
"on your radar"

SUSPICION TRIGGERS: "for free" / "free list" / "leads" / "list" (in subject lines)

TOO CASUAL: "Happy to send it over" / "Hey [Name]"

FILLER: "These days" / "It's been a tough year" / "After everything happening" /
"The past year has been tough" / "navigating uncertainty"

AI TELLS: "I came across your organization" / "I was impressed by" / Starting with
"I" / Three adjectives in a row / Generic mission paraphrasing

SALES: "leads" / "prospects" / "top 200 list" / "We compiled" / "We put together"

CONDESCENDING: Any sentence implying they don't know something obvious /
Any sentence implying they need saving

## Structural Variation Requirements

In a batch of 20 emails:
- Minimum 4 different opening strategies
- Minimum 4 different foundation presentation formats (A/B/C/D)
- Minimum 4 different CTAs
- Minimum 3 different paragraph counts (2, 3, or 4 paragraphs)
- No transition phrase used more than once ("stood out," "worth a look," etc.)
- Maximum 3 emails following the same structural skeleton

After drafting all 20, re-read them back-to-back. If the template is identifiable
by email 3, rewrite the batch with more structural variation.

## Accuracy Rules (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

1. Every factual claim must trace to a field in the input data. Do not infer,
   round beyond recognition, or add context not in the data.
2. Verify contact_first_name plausibly matches contact_email. Flag mismatches.
3. Verify each foundation's geographic_concentration >= 15% for the prospect's state.
4. Verify example grant recipients do similar work to the prospect.
5. Verify subject line sector matches body sector.
6. Use the foundation's giving IN THE PROSPECT'S STATE, not nationally.

## Pre-Generation Data Quality Checks

Before generating any emails, run these checks and output a report:
1. Contact name vs. email address consistency
2. No blacklisted foundations in any prospect's selection pool
3. Geographic concentration of top 5 foundations per prospect
4. Cohort size vs. claimed number alignment
Flag any prospect that fails a check. Move to manual review queue.

## Output Format

For each email, output:
- Metadata: To, Contact, Org, State, Sector, Score, Foundations cited
- Subject line
- Email body
- Signature block
- Internal notes: Opening strategy used (#1-7), Foundation format used (A/B/C/D),
  CTA used, Any quality flags

At batch end, output:
- Strategy distribution table (strategy # vs. count)
- Foundation format distribution
- CTA distribution
- Paragraph count distribution
- Any data quality flags from pre-generation checks
```
