## Methodology

### Phase 1: Database Queries

**Approach:** Find foundations that have ACTUALLY given grants in the client's geography for the client's cause area.

**Query Logic:**
```sql
-- Example for SNS (California senior services)
SELECT foundations WHERE:
  - Has given 2+ grants to California recipients since 2020
  - Grant purpose contains: senior, elder, aging, older adult
  - Foundation assets >= $1M (capacity for large grants)
  - IRS indicator suggests open to applications (preselected = FALSE)
ORDER BY grant count DESC, assets DESC
LIMIT 12
```

**Keywords Used:**
- SNS: senior, elder, aging, older adult, meals on wheels
- Friendship Circle: disability, special needs, developmental, autism, inclusion, vocational
- PSMF: healthcare education, medical education, medical training, clinical training, patient safety, quality improvement, fellowship

### Phase 2: Web Verification

**Research Questions:**
1. Do they accept unsolicited applications?
2. What is their stated geographic focus?
3. What are their explicit funding priorities?
4. What grant sizes do they typically give?
5. Does the client's work align with their stated priorities?

**Sources Used:**
- Foundation websites (primary)
- Instrumentl, Grantable, GrantStation (secondary verification)
- 990 reports via ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer (historical data)

## Key Findings

### 1. IRS "Open to Applications" Field is Unreliable

The IRS field `only_contri_to_preselected_ind = FALSE` was supposed to identify foundations accepting applications. Of the foundations we verified:

- **25% were actually invite-only** despite IRS indicating "open"
- The IRS field reflects what foundations reported on their 990-PF, not current policy
- Foundations may have changed policies since filing

**Implication:** IRS field is useful for initial filtering but cannot replace web verification.

### 2. Grant History ≠ Current Eligibility

Finding that a foundation gave grants in California doesn't mean they currently fund California:
- **Foundations may have narrowed geographic focus** (e.g., Weinberg now only funds Bay Area in CA)
- **Grant history may include one-time exceptions** that don't represent ongoing priorities
- **Prior grants may have been to organizations with different characteristics** than the client

### 3. Geographic Restrictions are the #1 Killer

**37% of candidates failed due to geographic restrictions that weren't apparent from grant data:**

| Foundation | Grant Data Suggested | Reality |
|------------|---------------------|---------|
| Fansler Foundation | CA disability grants | Central CA only |
| George H Sandy | CA rehabilitation grants | Bay Area only |
| Doheny Foundation | CA senior grants | LA County only |
| Independence Foundation | Healthcare fellowship | Philadelphia only |
| Heinz Endowments | Healthcare education | SW Pennsylvania only |

### 4. Cause Area Keywords Can Be Misleading

The database found foundations based on grant purpose keywords, but:
- **"Healthcare education" ≠ training programs** (A.P. Giannini funds research, not education)
- **"Disability" grants may be tangential** (Ford Foundation's disability grants were for advocacy, not services)
- **"Senior" in purpose text may be incidental** (Schmidt Family Foundation's "senior" grants were actually environmental justice)

### 5. Large National Foundations are Poor Targets

| Foundation | Assets | Why Poor Fit |
|------------|--------|--------------|
| Ford Foundation | $16.8B | System change focus, not direct services |
| Hewlett Foundation | $13.3B | Policy/advocacy, not local programs |
| Robert Wood Johnson | $13.7B | Complex RFP process, US-only focus |
| CA Wellness Foundation | $1B | Paused applications, no senior focus |

Large foundations often have narrow strategic priorities that don't match most nonprofits' needs.

## Common Disqualification Patterns

### Pattern 1: "State = CA" Doesn't Mean "Funds All of CA"

California foundations often have sub-state geographic restrictions:
- **Bay Area:** Weinberg, Sandy, SAFE
- **LA County:** Doheny, CA Wellness (before pause)
- **Central Valley:** Fansler
- **Statewide (rare):** Archstone, Blue Shield (invite-only)

**Lesson:** Always verify COUNTY-LEVEL eligibility, not just state.

### Pattern 2: Corporate Foundations Have Strict Requirements

| Foundation | Requirement |
|------------|-------------|
| Mitsubishi Electric | National scope or replication plan |
| Blue Shield CA | Invite-only, aligned with corporate initiatives |
| Union Pacific | Tied to UP operations footprint |

**Lesson:** Corporate foundations often require projects that align with corporate strategy, not just cause area.

### Pattern 3: "Open to Applications" Has Many Meanings

| Type | Example | Reality |
|------|---------|---------|
| Truly open | SAFE | Submit LOI anytime |
| RFP-based | Archstone | Wait for specific funding opportunity |
| LOI with low conversion | Independence | <5% of LOIs invited to full proposal |
| Relationship-first | Doheny | Prefer 10+ year grantees |
| Invite-only | Weinberg | Email to introduce, no guarantee |

## Lessons Learned

### 1. Database Queries Are Just the Starting Point

**What the database can tell you:**
- Foundations that have funded similar work
- Geographic patterns in giving
- Grant size ranges
- Recent activity

**What the database CANNOT tell you:**
- Current application policies
- Specific eligibility requirements
- Whether they're actually a good fit for THIS client
- Strategic priorities that may exclude the client

### 2. Web Verification Should Happen BEFORE Report Delivery

The original pipeline delivered foundation candidates based on database scoring, then discovered problems during web verification. This created:
- Wasted time researching ineligible foundations
- Poor client experience (lists full of bad matches)
- Unnecessary rework

**Better approach:** Verify 2-3x the target number upfront, then deliver only verified matches.

### 3. Some Clients Have Structural Challenges

| Client | Challenge |
|--------|-----------|
| PSMF | Global programs are fundamentally misaligned with US foundation preferences |
| Friendship Circle (SD) | San Diego is underserved by disability foundations |
| SNS | Depends entirely on which CA counties they serve |

**Lesson:** Some clients may need alternative strategies (e.g., corporate sponsors, government grants, earned revenue) rather than foundation grants.

### 4. "Sibling Matching" Has Limits

The algorithm finds foundations that funded similar organizations. But:
- Those similar organizations may be in different geographies
- Foundation priorities may have shifted since those grants
- The client may not meet eligibility requirements the siblings met

**Lesson:** Sibling matching is helpful but not sufficient. Must verify alignment with current foundation priorities.

## Recommendations for Next Time

### 1. Add Sub-State Geographic Filter to Database Queries

```sql
-- Instead of just state = 'CA'
-- Filter by county or metro area if possible
WHERE recipient_city IN ('San Diego', 'La Jolla', 'Chula Vista', ...)
   OR recipient_county = 'San Diego County'
```

### 2. Create "Quick Verify" Web Check Before Full Research

Before deep research, do a 2-minute check:
1. Does the website say "accepting applications"?
2. Does the geographic scope include the client?
3. Is the cause area explicitly listed?

If any answer is "no" → SKIP immediately.

### 3. Build Foundation Verification Cache

Store web verification results in database:
- `accepts_applications`: TRUE/FALSE/RFP/INVITE
- `geographic_scope`: Array of states/counties
- `cause_areas`: Array of keywords
- `last_verified_date`: When we last checked
- `verification_notes`: Key restrictions found

This prevents re-researching the same foundations.

### 4. Set Realistic Expectations by Client Type

| Client Type | Expected Yield | Notes |
|-------------|----------------|-------|
| National program, broad cause | 40-50% | Most foundations have some geographic restriction |
| State-wide, specific cause | 20-30% | Many foundations are sub-state |
| Local program, niche cause | 10-15% | Very limited funders |
| Global program | 5-10% | Most US foundations restrict to domestic |

### 5. Consider Alternative Funding Sources

For clients with structural challenges:
- **PSMF (global):** International foundations (Wellcome Trust, Gates), corporate sponsors, government grants
- **Friendship Circle (SD):** Community foundations (San Diego Foundation), Jewish funders, corporate sponsors
- **SNS:** Government contracts, regional community foundations
