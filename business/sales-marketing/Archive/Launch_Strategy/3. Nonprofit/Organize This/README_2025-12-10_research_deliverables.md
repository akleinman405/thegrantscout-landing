## KEY RESEARCH FINDINGS (SUMMARY)

### Finding 1: 80% of Foundations Have Capacity Programs
8 of 10 major community foundations have identified capacity-building initiatives, indicating growing sector priority.

### Finding 2: Vendors Are Actively Hired
5 of 8 foundations with programs explicitly hire external consultants, indicating healthy market for specialized expertise.

### Finding 3: Top Opportunities Are Clear
**San Antonio Area Foundation, Columbus Foundation, and East Bay Community Foundation** represent highest-confidence vendor opportunities based on:
- Explicit vendor hiring
- Formal program structures
- Visible budgets
- Demonstrated growth

### Finding 4: Market Size is Substantial
Estimated 150-200 nonprofit participants annually across these 10 foundations, implying $100K-$1.25M annual vendor spend (if vendors capture 50% of service delivery).

### Finding 5: Equity Focus is Strong
4 of 8 foundations emphasize racial equity, DEI, or Black-led organization capacity-building, indicating specialized opportunity for equity-focused vendors.

### Finding 6: Cohort Models Dominate
Most programs use cohort-based, high-touch models (5-15 organizations per cohort) rather than large-scale training, suggesting vendor model should be intensive rather than scalable.

### Finding 7: Affordability Matters
Most programs intentionally keep costs low ($980 for SAAFDN certificate as lowest example), suggesting vendor pricing should accommodate grant-funded budgets.

### Finding 8: Information Gaps Create Opportunity
2 foundations (Birmingham, Chattanooga) have limited public information on capacity programs, and 2 more (Lincoln, Wichita) have vague descriptions, creating need for vendor outreach to understand real opportunities.
