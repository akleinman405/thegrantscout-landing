# Community Foundation Capacity Programs Research - Final Summary

**Research Completion Date:** December 10, 2025
**Researcher:** Scout (Discovery Phase)
**Total Foundations Analyzed:** 10 Community Foundations
**Programs Identified:** 25+ distinct capacity-building initiatives

---

## RESEARCH OVERVIEW

This research project systematically investigated 10 major community foundations across the United States to identify, catalog, and assess their capacity-building, grantee support, and nonprofit training programs. The goal was to determine:

1. What capacity-building programs exist
2. Whether foundations hire external vendors/consultants
3. Contact information for program leadership
4. Program structure and scope (cohort sizes, deadlines, costs)
5. Vendor partnership opportunities

---

## KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: Capacity Building is a Growing Sector Priority

**Evidence:**
- 8 of 10 foundations (80%) have identified capacity-building programs
- Programs range from informal (workshops, resources) to formal (certificates, accelerators, multi-year cohorts)
- Foundations recently hired new leadership focused on capacity building (Columbus, CICF)
- Emerging infrastructure (new programs, centers) indicating growth

**Interpretation:** Community foundations are increasingly recognizing their role beyond grantmaking to building nonprofit sector infrastructure. This represents a significant market opportunity for capacity-building vendors.

---

### Finding 2: Vendor/Consultant Hiring is Explicit and Growing

**Confirmed Vendor Hiring Foundations:**
1. San Antonio Area Foundation - Explicit multiple vendor relationships
2. Columbus Foundation - Active vendor partnerships (Aly Sterling Philanthropy, consulting firms)
3. East Bay Community Foundation - Coaches and technical assistance providers
4. Community Foundation of Louisville - CNPE partnerships (Ashley|Rountree, Michele Shelton LLC)
5. Central Indiana Community Foundation - Fund Raising School at IUPUI, content creators

**Interpretation:** Community foundations do not attempt to provide all capacity-building services internally. They are actively seeking external expertise, indicating high vendor opportunity.

---

### Finding 3: Program Types Show Consistent Patterns

**Top Program Categories (by frequency):**
1. **Training & Certificates** (5 foundations) - SAAFDN, Columbus, CICF, others
2. **Capacity-Building Grants** (4 foundations) - Louisville, EBCF, Columbus, CICF
3. **Cohort-Based Learning** (4 foundations) - Louisville, EBCF, Columbus, SAAFDN
4. **Grantee Support/Resources** (8 foundations) - Nearly universal

**Content Themes (by frequency):**
- Board governance (7 foundations)
- Executive/leadership development (6 foundations)
- Fundraising/grant writing (5 foundations)
- Strategic planning (4 foundations)
- Financial management (4 foundations)
- DEI/Racial equity (4 foundations)

**Interpretation:** Consistent demand for these service areas suggests vendor specialization opportunities and proven market demand.

---

### Finding 4: Equity Focus is Strategic Priority

**Equity-Focused Programs:**
1. EBCF - ASCEND:BLO exclusively serves Black-led organizations
2. Louisville - Racial Justice Cohort, Black-led grantees focus
3. CICF - Racial Equity Fund for capacity-building
4. Columbus - Emerging leaders, underserved communities focus
5. SAAFDN - DEI training emphasis

**Interpretation:** Community foundations view equity capacity-building as distinct from general capacity-building. Vendors with racial equity expertise have competitive advantage.

---

### Finding 5: Information Gaps Correlate with Program Maturity

**High Public Visibility Programs:**
- San Antonio Area Foundation (explicit, detailed, multi-program)
- Columbus Foundation (structured, dedicated leadership, published deadlines)
- East Bay Community Foundation (specialized, marketed)

**Low Public Visibility Programs:**
- Birmingham, Chattanooga (no programs identified publicly)
- Lincoln, Wichita (vague program descriptions)
- Rochester (workshop series, limited detail)

**Interpretation:** Mature programs have marketing/communication infrastructure; emerging programs lack visibility. Opportunity to help foundations improve program promotion.

---

### Finding 6: Cohort Sizes Vary Significantly

**Small Cohorts (5 organizations):**
- EBCF ASCEND:BLO Accelerator (5 orgs)
- Louisville Alden Fellows (5 executives)

**Medium Cohorts (12-15):**
- Columbus Board Series (15 organizations)
- Louisville Racial Justice Cohort (12 organizations)

**Unspecified (Limited Seats):**
- SAAFDN programs ("limited seats" - not disclosed)

**Variable:**
- Columbus Summer Fellowship, forums (ongoing)

**Interpretation:** Small cohort sizes (5-15 orgs) suggest high-touch, intensive programs. Vendors should expect to serve 5-20 participants at a time, not large-scale programs.

---

### Finding 7: Cost Models Show Affordability Focus

**Lowest Cost:**
- SAAFDN Nonprofit Management Certificate: $980 (with payment plans)

**Grant/Free Models:**
- EBCF ASCEND:BLO: Grant-funded (free to participants)
- Columbus: Most programs grant-funded or free forums
- Louisville Fund for Louisville: Grant-funded

**Unknown/Variable:**
- Most other programs (details not disclosed)

**Interpretation:** Community foundations are intentionally keeping programs affordable for nonprofits. Vendors should expect lower fees than traditional consulting; offset with volume and grant funding.

---

### Finding 8: Program Maturity Varies by Foundation Size and Geography

**Most Mature Capacity Programs:**
1. San Antonio (largest, most diverse offerings)
2. Columbus (largest, formal structures)
3. East Bay (specialized, proven model)

**Emerging Programs:**
1. Central Indiana (new director just hired)
2. Louisville (transitioning to new model)

**Nascent/Unclear:**
1. Birmingham, Chattanooga (needs exploration)
2. Wichita, Lincoln (limited visibility)

**Interpretation:** Opportunity to serve foundations at different maturity levels with different offerings (implementation support, program design, vendor recruitment, etc.).

## CONCLUSIONS

This research identified significant and growing opportunities for capacity-building vendors in the community foundation space. While 8 of 10 major community foundations have active capacity-building programs, most explicitly hire external vendors and consultants, indicating a healthy market for specialized expertise.

**The most promising opportunities are with:**
1. San Antonio Area Foundation - Explicit vendor hiring, national leader status
2. Columbus Foundation - New infrastructure, dedicated leadership, formal budgets
3. East Bay Community Foundation - Proven accelerator model with external support

**Secondary opportunities exist with:**
4. Community Foundation of Louisville - Growing programs, partnership-based approach
5. Central Indiana Community Foundation - New capacity initiative with leadership change

**The research suggests community foundations view capacity-building as a distinct strategic priority**, separate from grantmaking, and are willing to invest in professional expertise to serve this mission. This represents a growing market for specialized nonprofit development services.
